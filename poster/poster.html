The Roles of Empathy and extroversion in L2 Spanish accent ability

Kyle Parrish1,


1 Department of Spanish and Portuguese, Rutgers University

Introduction

Previous work in monolinguals and bilinguals (Esteve-Gibert et al. 2020; Casillas et al. 2022) has shown that individuals with higher levels of empathy possess a perceptual advantage over their less empathetic peers. In particular, monolingual French speakers have shown evidence of better disambiguation of lexical meaning when only intonational, rather than lexical, cues were available (Esteve-Gibert et al. 2020). In L2 learning, English L1-Spanish L2 learners better distinguished questions from statements in their L2 when they had higher empathy and proficiency was held constant. Few studies have examined how individual differences in empathy are associated with L2 production. Lewandowski and Jilka (2019) found that the L2 speech of phonetically talented L2 English L1 German speakers who were more open, but not more empathetic, converged to a higher degree to a particular L2 dialect in conversation. The present study examined this question by measuring the accentedness and comprehensibility of spontaneous L2 Spanish production and each L2 speaker’s level of empathy and extroversion.

Research Question: Will more empathetic or extorverted L2 speakers be rated as less accented?

Methods

39 learners of L2 Spanish completed the Hunter Story Task used in previous studies (Huensch and Nagle 2021; Munro and Derwing 1995). Learners saw a series of pictures (Figure 1) and recorded a narration of the events pictured in the story. Empathy and extroversion were measured using two likert-style questionnaires, the Empathy Quotient questionnaire (Baron-Cohen and Wheelwright 2004) and the IPIP 50 item questionnaire (Goldberg 1992), 10 of which correspond to extroversion. Finally, L2 proficiency was measured using the Spanish LexTALE.

Figure 1: The Hunter Story prompt used in Munro & Derwing (1995) and Huensch & Nagle (2021)

Two utterances were extracted from each Hunter Story and presented to L1 speakers of Mexican Spanish (n = 103), who rated each utterance on a 1-9 continuous likert-style scale for accentendess and comprehensibility. In total, 103 raters generated 78 judgments each, for a total of 8034 data points for each accentedness and comprehensibility.

The data were analyzed using two Bayesian multilevel regression models, in which the outcome variables of each model were accentedness and comprehensibility ratings that were treated as continuous variables, since the continuous scale allowed for ratings to fall between whole numbers. In both models, each outcome was analyzed as a function empathy score, extroversion score, and proficiency (LexTALE score), with a random slope by participant for empathy and extroversion and a random intercept for rater.

Conclusion

There was no impact of empathy or extroversion on either accentedness or comprehensibility. The current data suggests that the perceptual advantage in the L1 and L2 does not extend to global production.
