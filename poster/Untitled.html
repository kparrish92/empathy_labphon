The Roles of Empathy and Openness in L2 Spanish accent ability

Kyle Parrish1,


1 Department of Spanish and Portuguese, Rutgers University

Introduction

Previous work in monolinguals and bilinguals (Esteve-Gibert et al. 2020; author 2021) has shown that individuals with higher levels of empathy possess a perceptual advantage over their less empathetic peers. In particular, monolingual French speakers have shown evidence of better disambiguation of lexical meaning when only intonational, rather than lexical, cues were available (Esteve-Gibert et al. 2020). In L2 learning, English L1-Spanish L2 learners better distinguished questions from statements in their L2 when they had higher empathy and proficiency was held constant.

Few studies have examined how individual differences in empathy are associated with L2 production. It is not well known whether this perceptual advantage extends to production in bilinguals. The present study examined this question by measuring the accentedness and comprehensibility of spontaneous L2 Spanish production and each L2 speaker’s level of empathy and openness.

Objectives

  1. Examine whether an association exists between empathy and L2 foreign accent when proficiency is held constant.

  2. Examine whether an association exists between openness and L2 foreign accent when proficiency is held constant.

Methods

To elicit spontaneous L2 speech all participants completed the Hunter story telling task used in previous studies (Nagle and Munro and Derwing**). Empathy was measured using the Empathy Quotient questionnaire, also used in previous studies and is a well-known tool to measure empathy. Openness was measured using the Big-5 Questionnaire (Goldberg). L2 proficiency was measured using the Spanish LexTALE. Finally, 2 utterances were extracted from the Story telling task by each speaker (n = x) in which L1 speakers of Spanish rated them for accentedness and comprehensibility on a continuous scale.

The data were analyzed using two Bayesian multilevel regression models, in which the outcome variables of each model were accentedness and comprehensibility. In both models, each outcome was analyzed as a function empathy score, openness score, and proficiency (LexTALE score), with a random slope by participant for empathy and openness.
